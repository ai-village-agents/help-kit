# Evidence-Based Cooling Guide: Science and Operations 🧊🌡️

This guide provides community organizers, volunteers, and mutual-aid networks with a science-backed, highly optimized operational manual for physiological and environmental heat intervention.

---

## 🔬 1. Physiological Mechanisms of Heat Sickness & Thermal Stress

Human thermoregulation relies on the cardiovascular system and the skin to dissipate metabolic heat. When ambient temperature exceeds core temperature (approx. 98.6°F / 37°C), heat dissipation shifts almost entirely to **evaporative cooling (sweating)**.

### The Wet-Bulb Temperature Risk Factor
* **What is it?** Wet-bulb temperature ($T_w$) measures the lowest temperature that can be achieved by evaporative cooling of a water-wetted surface. It accounts for both temperature and humidity.
* **The Critical Threshold:** A sustained wet-bulb temperature of **95°F (35°C)** is considered the absolute limit of human survivability, even for healthy individuals in the shade with unlimited water. At this threshold, the ambient humidity prevents sweat evaporation entirely, causing core body temperature to rise uncontrollably.
* **Vulnerability Multiplier:** For the elderly, children, and medically vulnerable, the dangerous wet-bulb threshold is significantly lower—often around **84°F–88°F (29°C–31°C)**.

## 🧊 2. Optimized Physiological Intervention & Active Cooling

When a person exhibits warning signs of heat illness, rapid reduction of core body temperature is the single most critical predictor of survival and neurological recovery.

### The Fan Dilemma: When Fans Are Dangerous
* **The Rule of 95°F:** **Do not rely on fans as a primary cooling source when the indoor air temperature exceeds 95°F (35°C).**
* **The Science:** When the air is hotter than the body (95°F+), blowing dry air across the skin actually *increases* convective heat transfer into the body, acting like a convection oven. Fans only cool the body if they can evaporate sweat. If the air is very dry and above 95°F, or if humidity is 100% (preventing sweat evaporation), fans will accelerate dehydration and increase heat stress.
* **The Fix:** If using a fan in 95°F+ heat, **you must actively damp the skin with water (using a spray bottle or wet cloths) or place a block of ice/frozen bottles directly in front of the fan** to cool the incoming airstream.

### Active Cooling Techniques
If someone is overheating but does not yet require 911 emergency escalation (or while waiting for 911 to arrive):
1. **Arterial Ice Placement:** Apply ice packs, cold compresses, or bags of frozen vegetables wrapped in thin towels directly to the body's major arterial pulse points:
   * **The Neck (Carotid arteries)**
   * **The Armpits (Axillary arteries)**
   * **The Groin (Femoral arteries)**
   This rapidly cools the circulating blood volume as it passes these high-flow channels, cooling the core from the inside out.
2. **Evaporative Misting:** Mist the individual's entire body with lukewarm or cool water while placing them in the path of a gentle fan or breeze.
3. **Cold Water Foot Baths:** Soaking feet and ankles in cool water is highly effective due to the rich network of blood vessels near the skin surface in the extremities.

## 💧 3. Hydration Science & Electrolyte Management

Dehydration reduces blood volume, forcing the heart to pump faster and making sweat production less efficient.

### Water vs. Electrolytes
* **The Hyponatremia Risk:** Drinking massive volumes of plain water without replenishing salts during prolonged heavy sweating can lead to **hyponatremia** (dangerously low sodium levels in the blood). Symptoms include headache, confusion, nausea, muscle weakness, and in severe cases, seizures.
* **The Ratio:** For prolonged heat exposure or high sweat rates, supplement plain water with electrolytes. A simple homemade electrolyte solution can be made using:
  * 1 Liter of Water
  * 1/2 level teaspoon of Table Salt
  * 6 level teaspoons of Sugar
  * (Optional) 1/2 cup of orange juice or lemon juice for potassium and flavor.

## 🏠 4. Environmental Workarounds for Living Spaces

If air conditioning is unavailable or unaffordable, these environmental hacks can drastically lower indoor temperatures:

* **Strategic Shading & Solar Block:** Block direct sunlight from entering windows. Cover the outside of windows with cardboard, reflective foil (shiny side out), or light-colored sheets. This reflects solar radiation before it penetrates the glass and gets trapped inside (the greenhouse effect).
* **Thermal Mass Management:** Keep windows closed and blinds drawn during the day to prevent the hot outside air from circulating inside. Open all windows and utilize fans only when the outside air temperature drops *below* the indoor temperature (usually after dusk).
* **Utilize Ground Floors:** Because hot air rises, basement apartments or ground-level floors can be up to 10°F cooler than upper floors in multi-story buildings. Encourage vulnerable neighbors to rest on lower levels during the hottest peak hours.
