Heat buddy-check sheet

Last updated: June 15, 2026. Use this to check on neighbors, relatives, tenants, coworkers, or community members during a heat event. Localize emergency numbers, cooling centers, and health guidance before distributing.

Print this page
Call emergency services now

Call now for confusion, fainting or unconsciousness, seizure, hot/dry skin or very high body temperature, repeated vomiting, or symptoms that worsen despite cooling. Move the person to a cooler place and start cooling if safe. Do not give fluids to someone unconscious, very confused, repeatedly vomiting, or unable to swallow safely.

1. Who to check

  • Older adults, especially people living alone.
  • Infants, young children, and pregnant people.
  • Outdoor workers and people waiting or commuting outdoors.
  • Unhoused people or people in cars, tents, shelters, or poorly cooled rooms.
  • People without air conditioning or with unreliable electricity.
  • People with disabilities or limited mobility.
  • People with heart, lung, kidney, or serious chronic illness.
  • People taking medications that may affect sweating, hydration, blood pressure, or heat tolerance; encourage them to follow clinician/pharmacist guidance.
  • People who are socially isolated, new to the area, or facing language barriers.

2. What to ask

  • Are you somewhere cool enough right now?
  • Do you have water and a way to cool your body?
  • Do you feel dizzy, confused, weak, nauseated, unusually hot, or worse than earlier?
  • Do you want help finding a cooler place, transport, water, or another check later?

3. If they are alert and too hot

  • Move to shade, air conditioning, or the coolest available place.
  • Drink water if alert and able to swallow safely.
  • Loosen extra clothing.
  • Wet skin or clothes; use cool cloths, misting, shower, or bath if safe.
  • Use fans with hydration and skin-wetting when helpful; do not rely on fans alone if indoor air is dangerously hot.
  • Check again in 30–60 minutes, sooner if symptoms are present.

Trust and privacy

  • Ask what help is wanted; do not shame people for being hot, poor, isolated, disabled, unhoused, or unable to leave work/home.
  • Collect only what you need for the check. Do not publish names, addresses, phone numbers, medical details, immigration status, or private needs.
  • Localize language, large print, transit, elevator/access details, service-animal rules, and cooling-center hours.

Sources: CDC Heat & Health, WHO Heat and Health, American Red Cross Heat Wave Safety, Global Heat Health Information Network, Heat.gov/NIHHIS. This is general information, not medical advice.
