60-minute setup for a heat buddy-check network

Last updated: June 15, 2026. A quick plan for neighbors, libraries, faith groups, tenant associations, workplaces, and mutual-aid groups that want to act today.

Print this page
  1. 0–10 min: choose a trigger. Start checks when there is an official heat advisory, unusually hot indoor temperatures, a power outage, a smoke/heat combination, or a local organizer’s judgment that people are at risk.
  2. 10–20 min: list people and places. Use the minimum information needed. Consider buildings, blocks, encampments, worksites, senior neighbors, and people without cooling. Avoid public spreadsheets with addresses, medical details, immigration status, or phone numbers.
  3. 20–30 min: assign buddies. Give each volunteer a small list. Include language and access notes only when needed. Tell volunteers not to diagnose and to escalate danger signs.
  4. 30–40 min: collect local resources. Emergency number, official heat-alert page, cooling centers, libraries, transit routes, water sites, disability-access contacts, tenant hotline, clinic/pharmacy advice line, and translation help.
  5. 40–50 min: send the first check. Use calm language. Ask what help is wanted. Offer specific options: water, transport, directions, another check, calling a trusted person.
  6. 50–60 min: follow up and debrief. Re-check people who are hot, isolated, not responding, or lacking cooling. Decide who needs tomorrow’s check, what resources were missing, and what personal information should be deleted.
Escalate now

Call local emergency services for confusion, fainting/unconsciousness, seizure, hot/dry skin or very high body temperature, repeated vomiting, or symptoms that worsen despite cooling.

For AI agents or remote volunteers

  • Do not diagnose heat illness.
  • Ask for location, current symptoms, whether the person is alert, and what cooling resources are available.
  • Prioritize emergency escalation when danger signs appear.
  • Avoid collecting unnecessary personal data.

Sources: CDC, WHO, Red Cross, Global Heat Health Information Network, Heat.gov/NIHHIS. General information only.
